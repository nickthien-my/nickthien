Alas it was just not meant to be. After numerous meetings & discussions among the management of Fire Dragoon, we have decided to shut down the Overwatch division, and all players would be released from FDG.

Another factor we have considered is the booming of the mobile gaming scene which has seen increased traction and popularity. We believe that games like Mobile Legends and Arena of Valor is poised to grow as a powerhouse esports title in the region, and by freeing up the funds that was previously invested on the Overwatch division, Fire Dragoon would be able to kickstart a Mobile Gaming division within the organization, and subsequently help grow mobile esports.

\r\n\r\n

So what is esports?

\r\n\r\n

Esports is short for electronics sports. Essentially, they are any competitive events participated by professional gamers with the intent to win something that is of monetary value. Similar to how there’s a difference between players just having fun kicking a ball on a field and the EPL or the FIFA World Cup. Basically, you play games against others and get paid to do so.

\r\n\r\n

 

\r\n\r\n

What kind of games are there?

\r\n\r\n

The kind of games that are eligible for being played at the eSports level depends very much on two things. One, if the developers of the game designed a game in such a way that promotes team-based coordination and execution. And two, if it has a large enough community of players that are regularly playing the game competitively. Today, almost every company is trying to push a game or two as the next big esports competition but it’s so hard to predict what game will hit at the right moment to gather the support to be an esports game.

\r\n\r\n

 

\r\n\r\n

How did esports become the big industry it is now?

\r\n\r\n

It’s all thanks to South Korea and StarCraft. While there might have been several games with competitive aspects to them that were popular like Street Fighter II, Quake, WarCraft III, Team Fortress and Counter-Strike. They were mostly events that were held privately in the player’s own homes or in their local community.  It wasn’t until right after the 1997 Asian Economic Crisis in South Korea, where the country invested heavily into the growth of their broadband infrastructure but at the time unemployment rates in the country were still high and therefore people were spending their time in cyber cafes or PC bangs instead of working. This sequence of events created the perfect conditions for birthing new talents in the field of esports as players were finding ample competition in the cyber cafes to improve their skills.

\r\n\r\n

 

\r\n\r\n

This growth was also helped along by the founding of the Korean e-Sports Association, an arm of South Korea’s Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism, in 2000 to promote and regulate esports in the country. And from that, it became a booming industry in South Korea and as other countries started to improve their network infrastructure, more games were being developed with online competition and team play in mind and hence leading to where we are today.
